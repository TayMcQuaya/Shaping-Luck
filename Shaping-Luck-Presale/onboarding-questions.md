# The 7 Onboarding Questions — Shaping Luck

These questions are designed to build a rich, multi-dimensional user profile in under 3 minutes. Each question targets a different facet of the person — career, personality, personal life, quirks, preferences — while lightly drawing from the Enneagram framework to capture core motivations and fears without ever feeling like a personality test.

The goal: **by the end, we know enough to start engineering serendipity for this specific human.**

---

## Question 1 — The Unfinished Sentence

**Dimension: Career & Purpose** | _Enneagram touch: Core desire_

> **"The thing I keep almost starting but never quite do is \*\***\_\_\_\***\*"**

**Format**: Open text (1-2 sentences)

**Why this works**: This reveals the gap between where someone is and where they want to be — the dormant ambition, the scary pivot, the creative project collecting dust. It's the single richest signal for serendipity because _this is where luck needs to land_. The Enneagram connection: what someone keeps avoiding tells you what they most desire (Type 3's unstarted venture, Type 4's unexpressed art, Type 7's commitment they keep dodging).

---

## Question 2 — The Energy Map

**Dimension: Personality & Engagement Style** | _Enneagram touch: Center of intelligence_

> **"When you walk into a room full of strangers, what's your first instinct?"**
>
> A) Scan for the most interesting person and make a beeline
> B) Find a quiet corner and observe before engaging
> C) Look for someone who seems uncomfortable and go help them
> D) Start a conversation with whoever's nearest — why not?

**Why this works**: This maps how someone interfaces with the unknown — which is literally what serendipity requires. It reveals whether they lead with gut (instinctive center), head (thinking center), or heart (feeling center) in the Enneagram model, without any jargon. Alex uses this to calibrate _how_ to present opportunities — some people need a bold push, others need a gentle breadcrumb trail.

---

## Question 3 — The Weird Flex

**Dimension: Quirks & Hidden Interests** | _Enneagram touch: Integration points_

> **"What's something you're unexpectedly good at or weirdly fascinated by that has nothing to do with your job?"**

**Format**: Open text (1-2 sentences)

**Why this works**: This is where the serendipity gold lives. The amateur mycologist, the person who can name every country's capital, the weekend blacksmith. These unexpected dimensions are _exactly_ what creates cross-domain connections — the secret ingredient that makes "lucky" encounters feel magical. In Enneagram terms, these often map to integration paths — the directions people grow toward when they're healthy and expanding.

---

## Question 4 — The Fork in the Road

**Dimension: Decision-Making & Values** | _Enneagram touch: Core fear vs. core desire tension_

> **"You get two job offers on the same day. One is safe, prestigious, and pays well. The other is risky, deeply exciting, and pays half. You have 24 hours. What do you actually do?"**
>
> A) Take the safe one — excitement fades, stability doesn't
> B) Take the risky one — I'll figure out the money
> C) Negotiate like hell to combine the best of both
> D) Call three people I trust and let their input tip the scales
> E) Stall for more time — 24 hours isn't enough for a decision this big

**Why this works**: This is a direct window into someone's relationship with fear and passion — the two axes of the Luck Quadrant. It also reveals their decision-making architecture (solo vs. communal, fast vs. deliberate, rational vs. intuitive). The Enneagram thread: each answer pattern maps loosely to how different types handle their core fear (security for Type 6, missing out for Type 7, being controlled for Type 8, etc.).

---

## Question 5 — The Dinner Table

**Dimension: Social Identity & Aspirations** | _Enneagram touch: Social instinct_

> **"You're hosting a dinner for 5 people. No small talk allowed, only real conversation. Who's at the table and what's the one topic you throw on it?"**

**Format**: Open text (names + topic)

**Why this works**: Who someone wants to sit with reveals who they want to _become_. The topic they choose reveals what they think matters most right now. Together, these create a map of aspirational identity and intellectual passion. This powers Alex's cross-domain matching — if you want to sit with Brené Brown and a marine biologist to discuss courage, Alex knows to surface opportunities at the intersection of vulnerability and exploration. The Enneagram lens: the social instinct variant shows up clearly here — some people pick world leaders, others pick their grandmother and a stranger from a podcast.

---

## Question 6 — The Contradiction

**Dimension: Self-Awareness & Inner Tension** | _Enneagram touch: Wings and stress arrows_

> **"What's a contradiction you live with? (Example: 'I crave adventure but always book the same vacation.' 'I love people but need to disappear for days.')"**

**Format**: Open text (1-2 sentences)

**Why this works**: This is the most psychologically revealing question in the set. Contradictions are where growth lives — and where the Enneagram framework is most useful. The tension between a person's dominant type and their wing, or their behavior under stress vs. growth, creates these lived paradoxes. For Alex, contradictions are _targeting data_: the person who craves adventure but plays it safe needs a low-risk gateway to something wild. The introvert who loves people needs curated small-group encounters, not networking events.

---

## Question 7 — The Lucky Break

**Dimension: Relationship with Luck & Openness** | _Enneagram touch: Stance (dependent, aggressive, withdrawing)_

> **"Think of the luckiest thing that ever happened to you. Now — what did you do, even unknowingly, that put you in the path of that luck?"**

**Format**: Open text (2-3 sentences)

**Why this works**: This is the capstone question because it teaches the framework _through the act of answering_. Most people have never reverse-engineered their own luck. By reflecting on it, they start to see the pattern: they said yes to something, showed up somewhere unexpected, talked to a stranger, followed a curiosity. This primes them for the entire Shaping Luck experience. For Alex, this answer reveals their _existing serendipity style_ — are they a connector, an explorer, a lurker, a builder? It also maps to the Enneagram stances: dependent types (1, 2, 6) often credit others, aggressive types (3, 7, 8) credit action, withdrawing types (4, 5, 9) credit being in the right internal state.

## How the 7 Questions Work Together

```
Q1 (Unfinished Sentence)  →  WHERE luck needs to go
Q2 (Energy Map)            →  HOW to deliver it
Q3 (Weird Flex)            →  WHAT unexpected connections to make
Q4 (Fork in the Road)      →  Their relationship with RISK
Q5 (Dinner Table)          →  WHO they aspire toward
Q6 (Contradiction)         →  WHERE they're stuck / ready to grow
Q7 (Lucky Break)           →  Their existing SERENDIPITY PATTERN
```

Together, these create a **Luck DNA** profile that is:

- Unique to each person (no two combinations produce the same output)
- Rich enough to power personalized serendipity matching from day one
- Light enough to complete in under 3 minutes
- Memorable enough that users talk about the experience itself

### Enneagram Integration (Subtle, Not Explicit)

The questions don't reference the Enneagram directly. Instead, they capture the same underlying dimensions:

- **Core desire and fear** (Q1, Q4)
- **Center of intelligence** — head, heart, gut (Q2)
- **Instinctual variants** — self-preservation, social, sexual/one-to-one (Q5, Q3)
- **Stress and growth arrows** (Q6)
- **Stance** — dependent, aggressive, withdrawing (Q7)

This means Alex can loosely map users to Enneagram patterns for coaching and matching without ever labeling them or making them feel boxed in. The framework is the engine under the hood, not the paint job.
