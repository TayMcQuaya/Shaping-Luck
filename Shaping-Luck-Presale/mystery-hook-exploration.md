# The Repeating Mystery Hook — Finding Our Line

> Hormozi's "better than an NFT but less than a Bitcoin" appears in EVERY email. We need ours.

---

## Why It Works for Hormozi

His hook has five properties that make it sticky:

1. **Specific enough to visualize** — you can picture an NFT, you can picture a Bitcoin
2. **Vague enough to not answer** — what sits between those two things? Nobody knows
3. **Creates social currency** — people text each other "what do you think it is?"
4. **Scales across contexts** — works in email subjects, P.S. lines, tweets, ads, conversations
5. **Has a payoff** — it gets revealed at the event, which gives people a reason to show up

The hook doesn't describe the product. It describes the _mystery of the product_. That's the distinction.

---

## What Our Hook Needs to Do

Our hook has a different job than Hormozi's. He's teasing a _thing_ (a giveaway). We're teasing an _experience_ (what happens when the system matches you with someone).

Our mystery isn't "what is the product?" — people already know it's a book and a webinar. Our mystery is **"what happens when it works?"** The thing people can't picture yet is what it feels like when engineered serendipity actually connects them with the right person at the right time.

So our hook should:

- Reference a **result** people experienced (social proof baked in)
- Be **emotionally specific** but **situationally vague** (you feel the impact but can't picture exactly how)
- Make people think **"I want that to happen to me"**
- Work as a P.S., a subject line fragment, a pull quote, and a verbal aside during the webinar

## The Candidates

### Category A: The Reaction Quote

These hooks center on what people _said_ after experiencing the matching system.

**A1: "How did you know?"**

> _"Every time we run the experiment, someone pulls us aside and asks the same question: 'How did you know?'"_

- Short form: _"They keep asking us: how did you know?"_
- P.S. form: _P.S. — We keep getting asked the same question. I'll tell you what it is on April 1st._
- Subject line variant: _"How did you know?"_
- Why it works: It implies the system paired someone with exactly the right person. "How did you know?" is something you'd say about a great gift, a great introduction, a great instinct. It positions the algorithm as intuition.
- Risk: Slightly cryptic on first read. Needs one sentence of context.

**A2: "I wasn't looking for her — but there she was."**

> _Already used as Email #3 subject. Could become the recurring thread._

- Short form: _"The people who weren't looking are the ones who found something."_
- P.S. form: _P.S. — There's a reason the best connections happen when you're not looking. We figured out why._
- Why it works: Already proven in Email #3. Romantic without being romantic. Universal.
- Risk: Gender-specific in its current form ("her"). Could generalize to "them."

**A3: "That's the person I've been needing to meet for ten years."**

> _Fabricated quote but represents the real reaction._

- Short form: _"Someone said that to us after 47 minutes."_
- P.S. form: _P.S. — It took 47 minutes. That's how long it takes when the system works._
- Subject line variant: _The person you've been needing to meet_
- Why it works: Incredibly specific. "Ten years" creates weight. "47 minutes" creates contrast. Together they imply the system collapses years of missed connections into one session.
- Risk: Feels manufactured if overused. Works best as a one-time reveal, not a recurring hook.

### Category B: The Paradox

These hooks create tension between two ideas that shouldn't coexist.

**B1: "We stopped networking. People started connecting."**

> _Short form: "What happens when you stop networking and start resonating?"_

- P.S. form: _P.S. — There's a word for what happens when you stop trying to network. I'll show you on April 1st._
- Subject line variant: _"Stop networking."_
- Why it works: "Stop networking" is counterintuitive for an audience of senior leaders who network constantly. It challenges their identity. And "resonating" hints at the Luck DNA framework without explaining it.
- Risk: Could read as anti-networking, which might alienate the LinkedIn crowd.

**B2: "Luck has a formula. The formula isn't what you think."**

> _Short form: "Luck has a formula."_

- P.S. form: _P.S. — We found the formula. It's not hustle, it's not manifestation, and it's not random. April 1st._
- Subject line variant: _"Luck has a formula."_
- Why it works: Direct challenge to the audience's mental model. Most high-achievers believe luck is either random or earned through hustle. Saying "there's a formula" is provocative. Saying "it's not what you think" keeps the mystery.
- Risk: Could sound like self-help clickbait. Needs Salim's credibility to land.

**B3: "600 people. One room. Zero small talk."**

> _Short form is the hook itself._

- P.S. form: _P.S. — 600 people. One room. Zero small talk. April 1st._
- Subject line variant: _"Zero small talk."_
- Why it works: Concrete. Visual. The audience hates small talk at conferences. This promises something fundamentally different. "600 people" establishes scale. "Zero small talk" establishes quality.
- Risk: Doesn't hint at the mechanism (the app, the matching). It sells the event but not the system. Works for Track F countdown but maybe not for the full campaign.

### Category C: The Tease

These hooks reference the system/app without naming it.

**C1: "Something we built knows who you should meet."**

> _Short form: "It already knows who you should meet."_

- P.S. form: _P.S. — We built something that knows. April 1st, you'll see how._
- Subject line variant: _"It knows who you should meet."_
- Why it works: Creepy-in-a-good-way. Implies AI + data + intuition. "It already knows" feels like the future. The word "should" (not "could" or "might") implies conviction.
- Risk: Could sound like surveillance tech or a dating app. Needs careful framing.

**C2: "We ran an experiment on 200 executives. They didn't know they were in it."**

> _Short form: "The experiment they didn't know they were in."_

- P.S. form: _P.S. — 200 executives. None of them knew what was happening. All of them felt it. April 1st, you'll understand._
- Subject line variant: _"The experiment no one knew about."_
- Why it works: Science + secrecy. "They didn't know they were in it" creates massive curiosity. "All of them felt it" implies the result was emotional and undeniable. This is the closest to Hormozi's structure — a mystery with built-in scale.
- Risk: "They didn't know they were in it" could read as manipulative without the right context.

**C3: "What if the right person is already in your orbit — and you've been looking past them?"**

> _Short form: "You've been looking past them."_

- P.S. form: _P.S. — The person who changes everything might already be in your orbit. On April 1st, I'll show you how we surface them._
- Why it works: Reframes luck as attention, not chance. Implies the system sees what you miss. Personal — everyone wonders if they're missing something.
- Risk: Long. Needs trimming to be a repeatable hook.

## The Recommendation: Layer Two Hooks

Hormozi has one product and one mystery, so one hook works. We have two mysteries:

1. **The system** — what we built (the app/matching engine)
2. **The result** — what happens when it works (the "how did you know?" moment)

### Primary hook (the thread): **"How did you know?"**

This is the line that recurs in every email. Sometimes as a P.S. Sometimes as a story intro. Sometimes as a pull quote. It's always italicized or set apart. It's always attributed to someone who experienced the system.

Why this one wins:

- It's a **question**, which is inherently engaging — the reader's brain tries to answer it
- It implies **a real person said this**, which is social proof without needing a testimonial
- It's **4 words** — short enough to be a subject line, a text, a whisper
- It positions the system as **intuitive**, not algorithmic — which is how Salim talks about luck
- It has a natural **payoff**: at the webinar, Salim can say _"I keep getting asked this question. Tonight I'll show you the answer."_

### Secondary hook (the payoff tease): **"600 people. One room. Zero small talk."**

This one is for Track F countdown and event-adjacent messaging. It sells the _experience_ of April 1st specifically. Use it in:

- Track F emails
- Social posts about the webinar
- The registration confirmation
- Any time you're selling the EVENT, not the system

## Alternative: If "How Did You Know?" Feels Too Soft

If the team wants something more provocative/punchy, the runner-up is:

**"We ran an experiment. Nobody knew they were in it. Everyone felt the result."**

This is closer to Hormozi's energy — bigger, bolder, slightly transgressive. It works well for a cold audience (Phase 2 especially) but may be too aggressive for Salim's warm list. Good backup for John's voice specifically.

---

## Decision Needed

Pick one. Thread it. Don't mix hooks — that's the whole lesson from Hormozi. One line, every email, building recognition until the audience can finish the sentence before they read it.

The recommendation: **"How did you know?"**

Four words. One question. Every email. Payoff on April 1st.
